# TPA Support Ruling — Zoning Rooms With No Internal Door Access (Garage Zone vs Day Conditioned)

## Summary

A Design Matters National TPA Support Desk email response to NRG ruling on how to zone rooms that have no internal door access to the main dwelling in NatHERS models: storage spaces for garden equipment and outdoor furniture (but not cars) may reasonably be entered as "Garage" zones because their thermal performance characteristics closely align with a garage — but if the room could function as a habitable room, it must be a day conditioned zone even without an internal connecting door. The screenshot is the response only; the original question is not visible.

## Key claims

- Question (implied from the response; original email not visible): how to zone a space with no internal door access to the main dwelling in a NatHERS model.
- Ruling: "Storage spaces for garden equipment and outdoor furniture but not cars have a thermal performance characteristics that are very closely aligned with a garage." (sic).
- Supporting reasoning: "They usually have smaller less leaky external doors than a garage and do not have carbon monoxide ventilation requirements."
- Ruling: "Accordingly it is reasonable to enter them in NatHERS models as 'Garage' zones."
- Limit on the ruling: "That would clearly not be the case if the room had the potential to provide the functionality of a habitable room even if there is no internal connecting door."
- "In that instance it would be a day conditioned zone." — i.e. habitable-capable rooms are day conditioned regardless of internal door access.
- The deciding test is potential functionality (habitable vs storage), not door connectivity to the main dwelling.
- Response issued by the Design Matters National TPA Support Desk, sent to NRG on Tuesday 28 Jul at 10:38 AM (year not shown); footer identifies Building Designers Association of Victoria Inc. T/as Design Matters National, ABN 36 982 485 577.
