# James Hardie Energy Efficiency Guide (July 2025)

## Summary

James Hardie's Energy Efficiency Guide (Australia, July 2025) is a manufacturer design handbook for reaching the NCC 2022 Part H6 7-star NatHERS requirement with Hardie cladding products — covering passive design principles (orientation, shading, glazing, insulation, thermal mass, ventilation, wall cavities, reflectivity, colour), city-by-city Level 1/2/3 specification tables for Melbourne, Sydney, Brisbane, Cairns, Adelaide, Perth and Hobart, costed upgrade hierarchies, and three worked case studies showing star-rating gains per upgrade. Useful for practical "what upgrade buys what" guidance, though it is vendor marketing material, not a regulatory document.

## Key claims

- NCC 2022 Part H6 (Clause H601 goals: reduce energy consumption and peak demand, reduce greenhouse gas emissions, improve occupant health and amenity) is met mainly via DTS provision H6D2 and Specification 42 clause S42C2 Heating and Cooling Loads: house energy rating software must show the design meets both the minimum star rating (7 stars, varying by state/territory) and the maximum heating/cooling loads from the ABCB Standard for NatHERS Heating and Cooling Load Limits.
- Queensland concession (per the guide, referencing QDC MP 4.1 Sustainable Buildings): a 1-star credit is available in climate zones 1 or 2 if the dwelling includes an outdoor living area per S42C2(3) of NCC 2022 Vol 2, the roof over it achieves total R-Value ≥1.5 for downward heat flow, and it has a ceiling fan with speed controller and blade diameter ≥900 mm.
- Optimum external wall R-value is climate/orientation dependent, typically between R2.0 and R2.7 as a system — beyond this the wall hits diminishing returns because heat reroutes through windows.
- Hardie wall system R-values (per AS/NZS 4859:2018): 8.5 mm Hardie cladding + Hardie Weather Barrier + R2.5 batts + 90x45 timber frame + plasterboard = R2.26 summer / R2.37 winter; adding 70x35 mm battens (cavity) = R2.69 summer / R2.84 winter; with R2.7 batts + cavity = R2.83 summer / R2.97 winter. Brisbane/Cairns Level 1 variant with R1.5/R2.0 batts = R1.93 summer / R2.04 winter.
- Hardie Wrap Weather Barrier: reflective surface can improve performance "up to 0.2 stars" when installed behind a drainage cavity; it is a water barrier and vapour-permeable membrane usable in climate zones 2-8.
- City climate zones: Sydney CZ5 (NatHERS CZ28 in case study), Brisbane CZ2, Melbourne CZ6 (NatHERS CZ21), Hobart CZ7, Perth CZ5, Adelaide CZ5, Cairns CZ1.
- Temperate-city design targets (Melbourne/Adelaide/Perth): living areas and main glazing to north; remove unnecessary south glazing and limit it to below 5% of house floor area; total room glazing below 15% of room floor area; reduce east/west glazing; double glazing to cut heat transfer.
- Hot-climate design targets (Brisbane/Cairns): cross-ventilation aligned to prevailing breezes; wide eaves/verandas/external shading (Cairns Level 3: >1,000 mm eaves); reflective insulation in walls and ceilings; LIMIT high thermal mass (lightweight construction preferred); light roof colours and ventilated roof spaces.
- Level 1→3 spec ladders per city cover windows (clear → low-e → thermally-broken/uPVC double glazing; Hobart starts at double glazing standard), external wall R-value (R1.5/R2.0 → R2.5 → R2.7-R3.0), ceiling insulation (R4.0 → R5.0 → R6.0), suspended floor insulation (up to R4.0+), slab insulation (waffle pod / raft slab variants with 25-50 mm slab-edge insulation; Hobart up to ≥R2.0 under-slab and edge), wall cavities, sarking reflectivity and colour tone.
- Melbourne Case Study 1: baseline 5.4 stars (heating load 66.3 vs max 48.0 MJ/m², cooling 5.4 vs max 41.0, NatHERS CZ21 — FAIL on stars and heating) upgraded to 7.2 stars (heating 35.8, cooling 21.9 — PASS) through a ~19-step hierarchy; the single largest gain (+0.6 stars) came from upgrading all glazing to double glazed; wet-area internal wall insulation added +0.2; heating was 70.6% of home energy usage.
- Sydney Case Study 2: baseline 5.5 stars (heating 51.5 vs max 56.0 — pass; cooling 40.6 vs max 37.5 — FAIL) upgraded to 7.1 stars (heating 22.7, cooling 23.9 — PASS); largest single gain (+0.5) was upgrading all glazing to double glazed; +0.4 from double glazed with single low-E.
- Melbourne Case Study 3 (same plot, same specs, different design): traditional House 1 scored 5.9 stars vs energy-efficient-design House 2 at 7.5 stars — 39.41% less energy (6,005 MJ/year saved) from design choices alone (north-facing living, garage/laundry buffering on south, removed terrace shading cover to expose slab thermal mass, minimal south/east/west glazing, zone-separating doors); House 2 reached 8.0 stars with two further upgrades (wet-area wall insulation +0.1; all windows to double-glazing + double low-E +0.4).
- Thermal mass guidance: effective only with correct solar exposure — expose to winter sun and shade in summer; misplaced thermal mass (no sun exposure next to a cold area, or over-exposed next to a hot area) actively worsens performance.
- Colour: light colours absorb least heat (cooler surfaces), dark colours (e.g. Colorbond Monument, Basalt) absorb most — most important on roofs; recommendation logic is dark tones for houses that run cold, light for houses that run hot.
- On double-storey dwellings, adding fall-protection screens to upper-storey windows enables full window openability, increasing cross-flow ventilation and potentially reducing cooling loads.

## Caveats

- Manufacturer marketing/technical literature (James Hardie, July 2025) — design recommendations and "star improvement" figures are indicative examples favouring Hardie products, not regulatory values; every project requires assessment by a qualified energy efficiency assessor (the guide itself says so).
- Case-study star gains are project-specific; the guide's own footnote states the upgrade hierarchies "do not guarantee specific performance outcomes".
- Some table layouts (Level 1/2/3 matrices, case-study upgrade/star tables) degraded in extraction; verify exact pairings of upgrade-to-star-gain against the source PDF before quoting precisely.
