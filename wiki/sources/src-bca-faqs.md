# NCC Resource Kit — BCA Volumes One and Two FAQs (2016)

## Summary

A 2016 ABCB NCC Resource Kit document answering 14 frequently asked questions about the National Construction Code and Building Code of Australia — what the NCC is, who produces it, its legal status, the performance-based compliance structure, Performance vs Deemed-to-Satisfy Solutions, building classifications, state variations and defined terms. Introductory training material for new NCC users, not a technical reference.

## Key claims

- The NCC is a COAG initiative incorporating all on-site construction requirements into a single code: the BCA (Volumes One and Two) plus the Plumbing Code of Australia (Volume Three).
- Each State and Territory adopts the NCC into legislation as the technical basis of building and plumbing regulation, so the NCC is law throughout Australia (subject to any legislated dispensation processes).
- The NCC is produced by the Australian Building Codes Board (ABCB), comprising an independent Chairman, representatives of all three levels of government, and ministerially appointed industry members.
- The mandatory parts of the NCC are the Performance Requirements — Sections B to J of Volume One and Section 2 of Volume Two; DtS Solutions are an optional means of compliance.
- Compliance can be achieved by (a) a Performance Solution, (b) a DtS Solution, or (c) a combination — per Clause A0.2 (Vol One) and 1.0.2 (Vol Two) — allowing a Performance Solution for only a small part of a design.
- Performance Solutions must be assessed using evidence of suitability, verification methods, comparison with the DtS Provisions, and/or expert judgement.
- Volume Two's DtS Provisions are called "Acceptable Construction": acceptable construction manuals (e.g. referenced Australian Standards) and acceptable construction practices (traditional techniques described in the NCC); both are optional.
- Generally all new building work must comply with the NCC, including alterations/additions; a change of use/classification generally triggers compliance as if a new building (though the Certifying Authority may require only parts to comply), and some jurisdictions allow authorities to require existing buildings to be upgraded (most likely for essential fire safety).
- State/Territory variations and additions exist because those governments retain the right to vary or add to the NCC; they are law within the respective jurisdiction.
- Building classifications group buildings by dominant characteristics and risk (activities, occupant characteristics, fuel load); italicised terms in the NCC are defined terms whose meanings may differ from common usage.

## Concepts touched

NCC, building classifications, Performance Solutions, building act, alterations, training

## Caveats

- 2016 publication referencing pre-NCC 2019 structure (Clause A0.2/1.0.2; "Alternative Solution"-era framing): the governance and compliance concepts remain accurate in substance, but clause references were renumbered in NCC 2019/2022 (e.g. A0.2 → A2G1/A2G2 area of Section A).
- Training/awareness material — contains no technical values.
