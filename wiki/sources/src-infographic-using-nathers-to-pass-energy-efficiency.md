# Using NatHERS to Pass NCC Energy Efficiency Requirements — Infographic

## Summary

One-page NatHERS infographic showing the two-step compliance sequence for passing the NCC energy efficiency requirements: first achieve the thermal shell star rating at or above the NCC minimum, then add appliances and onsite energy generation to produce the Whole of Home rating out of 100. It is the simplest visual explainer of how the thermal rating and Whole of Home rating fit together.

## Key claims

- Step 1: first achieve a NatHERS thermal rating (stars out of 10) at the minimum or above the NCC requirement; the thermal rating is based on the home's design, construction materials and the climate where it is built, and assesses heating and cooling needs of the construction.
- Step 2: once the thermal rating is established, add home appliances and onsite energy generation to generate the Whole of Home rating out of 100.
- It is an NCC requirement to meet or exceed the minimum Whole of Home rating.
- Whole of Home inputs shown: heating and cooling appliances, hot water, lighting, pool/spa equipment, and onsite energy generation and storage.
- Illustrative figures: 7.0 star thermal shell with 107.9 MJ/m2 predicted annual heating/cooling energy load (standard occupancy assumptions), and a Whole of Home rating of 60 out of 100 on a scale running to "Net zero home".

## Concepts touched

NatHERS, Whole of Home, 7-Star, NCC 2022, thermal shell, appliances, solar PV, pool and spa equipment

## Caveats

- Infographic only — the 7.0 star / 107.9 MJ/m2 / 60-out-of-100 values are illustrative sample figures, not requirements.
- No NCC edition is stated on the page; the Whole of Home requirement it describes was introduced in NCC 2022.
