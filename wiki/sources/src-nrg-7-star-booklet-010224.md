# NRG Efficient Homes 7 Star Information Booklet (1 February 2024)

## Summary

NRG Efficient Homes' own client-facing information booklet (dated 1 February 2024) introducing the NCC 2022 7-star NatHERS requirement and Whole of Home ratings, NRG's preliminary assessment service offer and pricing, and the full checklist of information clients must supply for a Whole of Home assessment. This is NRG's primary handout for explaining the 7-star transition to builders and designers; an earlier co-branded variant exists as [[src-nrg-7-star-booklet-ausnviro]].

## Key claims

- NCC 2022 requires a new minimum thermal performance rating of 7 stars under NatHERS, and all new homes must also meet a minimum annual energy use budget.
- The NCC 2022 changes cover: building shell minimum raised from 6 to 7 stars (out of 10); a Whole-of-Home annual energy use budget for fixed appliances (hot water, heating and cooling, lighting, pool and spa pumps) with potential rooftop-solar offset; removal of the Victorian variation requiring a solar water heater or rainwater tank; and requirements that apartments be designed for easy, cost-effective retrofit of solar and EV charging.
- Key differences between a 6-star and 7-star home: better design/orientation to the sun (passive winter heating, summer shading), improved ceiling/wall/under-floor insulation for the climate, and high performance glazing.
- Timing as stated in this edition: the 7-star requirement was to be mandatory from 1 October 2023 but "has now been extended to 2024" — readers are referred to NRG's current state-to-state summary for details.
- NRG's operational position: the majority of dwellings NRG assesses would NOT achieve 7 stars even with major insulation and glazing upgrades — described as the industry consensus from discussion and surveys.
- NRG strongly suggests all new projects be sent through for a preliminary assessment at concept stage to find major issues early, reduce upgrade costs and confirm the design can achieve minimum compliance before BA approval stage.
- Preliminary assessments are priced at $250-$400 + GST per dwelling.
- The Whole of Home rating (0-100) covers energy used by heating, cooling, cooking and plug-in appliances, hot water systems, lighting, and pool and spa equipment, minus energy generated by solar panels — demonstrating how the home meets the NCC energy use budget.
- Information clients must provide for a WoH assessment (on plans or in a specification): heating/cooling appliance types, efficiencies and a mark-up of conditioned zones served; hot water service type and efficiency; calculated lighting power density (W/m2) complying with Section 13.7.6 of the ABCB Housing Provisions; pool dimensions (surface area m2 or volume in litres), pump model/type (single-, double- or multispeed), cleaner type and pump star rating (energy rating label from 1 October 2022); PV array rated sizes (kW), azimuth and inclination per array, inverter size (kW), phases and export limits (kW).
- If any required information is not provided, software defaults are used, which could affect the compliance outcome.

## Caveats

- NRG's own marketing/educational material (1 February 2024 edition), not a regulatory document; the "extended to 2024" timing claim is NRG's summary and should be checked against each state's actual adoption dates.
- Staged text contains only the three NRG-authored pages; if the published booklet appends third-party fact sheets (as the Ausnviro variant does), those did not extract — verify against the source PDF.
- Updates and supersedes the June 2023 Ausnviro co-branded edition ([[src-nrg-7-star-booklet-ausnviro]]): rebranded from Ausnviro to NRG Efficient Homes, mandatory date updated from "1 October 2023" to "extended to 2024", and preliminary assessment pricing made explicit ($250-$400 + GST) where the earlier edition said "quoted per dwelling".
