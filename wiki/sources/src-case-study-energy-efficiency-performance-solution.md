# ABCB Case Study — NCC Volume Two Energy Efficiency Performance Solution for P2.6.1, Climate Zone 1 (2016)

## Summary

Non-mandatory ABCB case study (August 2016, NCC 2016) walking through the development of a Performance Solution against NCC Volume Two Performance Requirement P2.6.1 for a Class 1a passive/hybrid tropical house in NCC climate zone 1 (Top End, NT) — a 113 m², three-bedroom timber-framed dwelling where only the bedrooms can be air-conditioned. It applies the ABCB four-step Performance Solution process (design brief, analysis, evaluation, final report) using expert judgement as the assessment method, and demonstrates psychrometric/comfort analysis, passive design documentation, and comparative R-value and glazing calculations against the DtS elemental provisions. Includes appendices on passive design principles, free-running/conditioned/hybrid design types, and the NCC compliance structure.

## Key claims

- For free-running or hybrid designs, a Performance Solution is likely the only option to satisfy P2.6.1, because the Volume Two DtS provisions were developed primarily for dwellings assumed to have artificial heating/cooling.
- The solution uses the ABCB four-step Guidance Document process: (1) performance-based design brief (PBDB) with stakeholders, (2) analysis/modelling/testing, (3) collate and evaluate results, (4) final report; the assessment method chosen is Expert Judgement per NCC Part 1.0.5, with the building designer as the expert.
- Agreed acceptance criterion: thermal comfort range 22.8–29.4°C, derived from the ASHRAE Standard 55-2013 adaptive comfort model for naturally ventilated buildings at a 90% acceptability limit; occupant clothing 0.6–1.2 clo; metabolic rates up to 2.0 met (116 W/m²).
- Ceiling fans are provided in all habitable rooms with an operating air velocity of 3.6 m/s; the physiological cooling effect is approximated as 6 × ws − ws² °C (ws = air speed m/s) — e.g. 1 m/s air movement makes 32°C feel like about 27°C; 1 m/s is tolerable, ~1.5 m/s the upper limit for normal occupancy.
- Climate analysis uses BOM data and microclimate observation plotted on psychrometric charts (Climate Consultant 6.0) against ASHRAE 55-2013 comfort zones to justify shading, ventilation and the no-heating position (climate zone 1 requires cooling only, so P2.6.1(e)(i) "solar radiation utilised for heating" does not apply).
- Passive design measures: east–west orientation with envelope mostly facing north/south; large roof overhangs fully shading walls year-round (effective shading can block up to 90% of solar heat); louvre windows (about 80% openable area vs 50% for casement) for cross-ventilation, including clerestory louvres to exhaust hot air; elevated lightweight construction.
- The bedrooms (the only conditioned spaces) are treated as sealed, insulated "boxes": insulation forming a continuous blanket per the intent of DtS Parts 3.12.1.1 and 3.12.3, with sealing of penetrations (downlights, fans), roofs, walls, floors and frames.
- Roof insulation comparison with DtS: required Total R-Value 4.1 (Table 3.12.1.1a, downward heat flow); skillion roof construction R 0.48 plus reflective insulation membrane R 1.28 gives 1.76, so added insulation of at least R 2.34 is required.
- Wall comparison: required Total R-Value 2.4 (Table 3.12.1.3a); fibre-cement on timber frame construction R 0.42 minus wall cavity R 0.17 leaves 0.25, so added insulation of at least R 2.15.
- Floor comparison: required Total R-Value 1.5 (Table 3.12.1.4); unenclosed suspended timber floor R 0.39 (upward heat flow), so added insulation of at least R 1.1.
- Roof colour: a light grey roof with a solar absorptance of 0.45 is used; a value of less than 0.4 typically corresponds to white/off-white/cream; reflective foil insulation is preferred in the tropics (reduces daytime radiant gain while allowing night-time heat loss), and bulk insulation should be used carefully in cooling-dominated climates.
- Glazing: single glazing is accepted in non-conditioned spaces because all glazing is fully shaded; bedroom glazing is assessed using the ABCB Glazing Calculator applied per individual air-conditioned space — a deliberate departure from the normal Part 3.12.2.1 per-storey application.
- Validation includes stakeholder review and, where the approval authority lacks expertise, peer review by a suitably competent independent person; Queensland is noted as having a formal energy assessment peer review process.
- Final report contents: cover letter with PBDB overview, construction specifications (AC location, glazing/window types, ceiling fan locations and speeds, insulation/membrane specs), drawings (site analysis, shading projections, air-flow and conditioned-space diagrams) and calculations (air flow, glazing).
- P2.6.1 (reproduced in Appendix C) requires thermal performance facilitating efficient use of energy for artificial heating and cooling appropriate to: function/use, internal environment, geographic location, nearby permanent features, solar radiation (utilised for heating, controlled for cooling), envelope sealing, and air movement for cooling.

## Caveats

- Written against NCC 2016 Volume Two: P2.6.1 is now H6P1 (NCC 2022), Part 3.12.x is now ABCB Housing Provisions Section 13, and the cited R-value/glazing tables and 6-star-era settings are superseded by the 7-star framework — use the method, not the numbers, for current work.
- Non-mandatory guidance; acceptance of any such Performance Solution rests with the appropriate approval authority and State/Territory legislation (it expressly does not demonstrate full NCC compliance).
- Several figures (plans, psychrometric charts, glazing calculator outputs) are images that did not survive text extraction; the R-value tables transcribed above were clear, but verify against the source PDF before reuse.
