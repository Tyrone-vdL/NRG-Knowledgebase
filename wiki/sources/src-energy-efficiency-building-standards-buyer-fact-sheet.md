# Energy Efficient New Homes — Victorian Homebuyer Fact Sheet (7 Star Standards)

## Summary

Victorian Government (DEECA, 2023) fact sheet for homebuyers explaining the NCC 2022 7-star and Whole-of-Home energy standards that became mandatory from 1 October 2023, including benefits, the annual energy use budget mechanism, cost impacts and a real 7.1-star case-study home. It frames compliance as three steps: build to a 7-star thermal shell, choose efficient fixed appliances, and install rooftop solar to offset remaining usage.

## Key claims

- Three ways to meet the new standards: (1) design/build to a minimum 7-star thermal performance rating, (2) choose more energy efficient fixed appliances, (3) install rooftop solar to offset remaining energy usage and meet the Whole-of-Home budget.
- Future-proofing suggestions: build all-electric, install batteries, use energy monitoring/timers, efficient plug-in appliances, and select at least 4 star WELS hot water fixtures (showers, washing machines).
- Research shows cost savings of $300 to over $1,000 a year are achievable through energy efficient homes; NCC 2022 analysis showed at least $300/yr for an average Victorian household, and Renew analysis found $1,056/yr for an all-electric home in Melbourne.
- Hot water, heating, cooling and lighting account for 70-80% of an average home's energy use.
- Residential buildings are responsible for 11% of Australia's greenhouse gas emissions.
- The Whole-of-Home annual energy usage budget is met through a flexible combination of: type/efficiency of fixed appliances (hot water, heating, cooling, lighting, pool and spa pumps) and rooftop solar to offset above-budget use; builders can choose fuel type (gas or electricity).
- NCC 2022 energy efficiency provisions were available from 1 October 2022 with a 12-month transition (homes could be built to NCC 2019 or NCC 2022); the new standards became mandatory from 1 October 2023.
- Achieving 7 stars need not add significant cost if smart design is considered early; the Victorian Government funds rebates under the Solar Homes Program to reduce cost impacts.
- Victoria's targets: net-zero emissions by 2050, with a 45-50 per cent reduction on 2005 levels by 2030.
- Case study (Henley 2022 Good Friday Appeal Charity home, double storey Vogue 34): NatHERS rating 7.1 stars, 257.3 m2, 4 bed/3 bath, ducted reverse cycle heating/cooling, heat pump hot water, induction cooking, 6 kW solar PV, 13.5 kWh battery, annual energy bill $591.

## Concepts touched

## Caveats

- Victorian Government publication (DEECA, 2023, ISBN 978-1-76136-078-7) — savings figures, rebates (Solar Homes Program) and emissions targets are Victoria-specific; other jurisdictions differ.
- Consumer-facing; transition dates given are the national/Victorian NCC 2022 timeline and do not cover other states' deferrals.
