# Keeping Our Stormwater Clean — A Builder's Guide (EPA Victoria / Melbourne Water)

## Summary

"Keeping Our Stormwater Clean — A Builder's Guide" (EPA Victoria / Melbourne Water, fourth edition October 2006) gives builders six site rules for controlling sediment, litter and wash-water pollution from residential building sites to comply with Victorian state and council stormwater regulations, with build specifications for sediment control fences, gravel sausages and stabilised crossovers, plus a photocopiable Site Management Plan and Clean Site Checklist. Relevant to SDA/WSUD obligations on construction sites; penalties apply for polluting stormwater, and responsibility sits with the developer or person managing the site.

## Key claims

- Six site rules: (1) check council requirements and plan before starting work; (2) stop erosion and contain sediment on site; (3) protect/contain stockpiles on site; (4) keep mud off the road and on site; (5) keep litter contained on site; (6) clean and wash up on site.
- Stormwater is not treated and carries pollution directly to local waterways and bays; sediment from building sites polluting stormwater is an offence under state and local council laws, with penalties; the developer or person managing the site is responsible.
- Sediment escaping sites makes roads/footpaths slippery (public liability risk), clouds waterways killing plants and animals, blocks stormwater pits and pipes (flooding risk), and overloads stormwater filtration systems such as raingardens and swales.
- Plan around the site's lowest point: water runs to it — place the crossover as far from it as possible, stockpiles away from it, sediment control fences along the lowest boundary, and keep a vegetation buffer there. Install the sediment fence BEFORE the temporary site fence.
- Sediment control fence build spec: geotextile filter fabric required (shade cloth is NOT suitable); trench 150 mm deep to bury the fabric base; 1.5 m (1500 mm) posts driven 600 mm into the ground — star pickets at maximum 2 m spacing, 38 mm wooden posts at 1.2 m intervals (max 2 m); fabric fixed to the upslope side of posts with cable ties or staples; joins only at pickets with 150 mm overlap; fence height 900 mm max; an extra star picket 1.5 m upslope every 20 m spreads water volume; turn ends upslope to allow ponding; remove silt deposits after storms.
- Early downpipe connection: connect downpipes to stormwater or on-site detention as soon as the roof is installed (temporary or permanent) — fewer drainage problems, less mud, safer site, less foundation damage, less downtime. If connection isn't possible, pipe roof water onto a well-vegetated or bunded area.
- Gravel sausage spec: geotextile sleeve filled with 25-50 mm gravel, 150 mm high, placed tight against kerbing on the upslope side of the inlet pit and extending beyond the grate, with a 100 mm gap (held by wooden blocks) between pit front and sausage; clean out regularly; never hose sediment down the gutter.
- Cutting dust/slurry control: cut materials in a set area on site away from stormwater drains; place sediment logs downslope; cement/concrete slurry changes water acidity and can kill aquatic life; sweep up slurry and bin it — DO NOT hose slurry away.
- Stockpiles: store on site, upslope of the sediment fence, covered with a tarp if exposed for some time; never on footpath, gutter or road — off-site storage needs council permits, with tarps/pallets/skips and sediment logs around the downslope base.
- Mud control: build a crushed rock crossover — remove a strip of soil ≥3 m wide from the road (or where the concrete crossover ends) to the nearest building point or a minimum of 5 m; use road base, 40 mm aggregate or crushed rock to 200 mm depth; restrict vehicle access to this point; remove mud from truck tyres with a shovel before leaving; if mud reaches the road, remove it with broom or shovel — never a hose.
- Litter: mesh bins (50 mm mesh or smaller) with closeable lids for large items; smaller bins for paper/wrappers; empty regularly; site fencing helps contain litter and provide security but A FENCE DOES NOT NEGATE THE NEED FOR A BIN.
- Washing up: use a set bunded wash-up area with wash-out barrels away from stormwater drains (can share the cutting area); sediment fences will NOT stop chemicals; concrete slurry can be tipped into a plastic/geotextile-lined ditch, with hardened solids binned or recycled as road base; paint washing uses a wash container and a rinse container with overnight settling; oil-based paints washed in solvent baths — solvent must go to a waste disposal company, never on the ground.
- Includes a photocopiable Site Management Plan template (legend: bin, grass filter strip, rumble grid, silt fence, skip, stabilised access point, stockpile, temporary fencing, retained vegetation, wash-up area, gravel sausage) and a Clean Site Checklist covering all six rules.
- Indicative supplier pricing (2006): silt fence ~$125 per 100 m supply; geofabric fencing $55-130 per 100 m roll; installed silt fence ~$215-220 for a 20 m frontage; temporary flexible downpipe kit $135 (does 2-3 16-square houses); environmental settlement tank hire $542/week (4 m tank).

## Caveats

- Victorian publication (EPA Victoria, Melbourne Water and partner councils); fourth edition, revised October 2006 — supplier prices and contact details are nearly two decades old and obsolete; the regulatory framework (e.g. Victorian EPA general environmental duty under the Environment Protection Act 2017) has changed since publication, though the practical site controls remain standard practice.
- Check the relevant local council's current sediment and erosion control requirements before relying on specific dimensions or permit advice.
